  • The Equation for a Harmonic-Oscillator Model of a Diatomic Molecule Contains the Reduced Mass of the Molecule

The quantum-mechanical description of vibrational motion using the harmonic oscillator model will produce vibrational quantum numbers, vibrational wavefunctions, quantized vibrational energies, and a zero-point energy. We start by writing the (time-independent) Schrödinger equation for the harmonic oscillator,

Ĥ (Q)=12μp̂ 2Q+k2Q̂ 2(5.6.1)(5.6.1)H^(Q)=12μp^Q2+k2Q^2

where the independent variable, QQ, is the length of the normal coordinate, which represents the displacements of all the atoms in a molecule from their equilibrium positions in a particular normal mode. For the case of a diatomic molecule, QQ is the change in the bond length from its equilibrium value.

For a diatomic molecule, there is only one vibrational normal mode, so there will be only a single set of wavefunctions and energy levels for this system. For more complex molecules, there will be a set of wavefunctions and energy levels associated with each normal mode. Based on our experience with the particle in a box, we anticipate that the index vv in Equation 5.6.15.6.1 will turn out to be the quantum number that identifies each wavefunction and its corresponding vibrational energy.

Be careful not to confuse vv with the Greek symbol for frequency νν.

The Hamiltonian operator, the general quantum mechanical operator for energy, includes both a kinetic energy term, T̂ T^, and a potential energy term, V̂ V^.

Ĥ =T̂ +V̂ (5.6.2)(5.6.2)H^=T^+V^

For the free particle and the particle in a box, the potential energy term used in the Hamiltonian was zero. As shown in Equation 5.6.25.6.2, the classical expression for the energy of a harmonic oscillator includes both a kinetic energy term and the harmonic potential energy term. Transforming this equation into the corresponding Hamiltonian operator gives,

Ĥ (Q)=12μP̂ 2Q+k2Q̂ 2(5.6.3)(5.6.3)H^(Q)=12μP^Q2+k2Q^2

where Q̂ Q^ is the operator for the length of the normal coordinate, and P̂ QP^Q is the momentum operator associated with the normal coordinate. μμ is an effective (reduced) mass, and kk is an effective force constant, and these quantities will be different for each of the normal modes (vibrations).

Substituting the definitions for the operators yields

Ĥ (Q)=−ℏ22μd2dQ2+k2Q2(5.6.4)(5.6.4)H^(Q)=−ℏ22μd2dQ2+k2Q2

since the operator for position or displacement is just the position or displacement. The Schrödinger Equation (Equation 5.6.15.6.1) then becomes

−ℏ22μd2ψv(Q)dQ2+k2Q2ψv(Q)=Evψv(Q)(5.6.5)(5.6.5)−ℏ22μd2ψv(Q)dQ2+k2Q2ψv(Q)=Evψv(Q)

or upon rearranging

d2ψv(Q)dQ2+2μℏ2(Ev−k2Q2)ψv(Q)=0(5.6.6)(5.6.6)d2ψv(Q)dQ2+2μℏ2(Ev−k2Q2)ψv(Q)=0

This differential equation is not straightforward to solve. Rather than fully develop the details of the solution, we will outline the method used because it represents a common strategy for solving differential equations. The steps taken to solve Equation 5.6.65.6.6 are to simplify the equation by collecting constants in the parameter ββ

β2=ℏμk‾‾‾√(5.6.7)(5.6.7)β2=ℏμk

and then changing the variable from QQ to xx where

x=Qβ(5.6.8)(5.6.8)x=Qβ

so that

d2dQ2=1β2d2dx2(5.6.9)(5.6.9)d2dQ2=1β2d2dx2

After substituting Equations 5.6.75.6.7 and 5.6.95.6.9 into Equation 5.6.65.6.6 the differential equation for the harmonic oscillator becomes

d2ψv(x)dx2+(2μβ2Evℏ2−x2)ψv(x)=0(5.6.10)(5.6.10)d2ψv(x)dx2+(2μβ2Evℏ2−x2)ψv(x)=0

A common strategy for solving differential equations, which is employed here, is to find a solution that is valid for large values of the variable and then develop the complete solution as a product of this asymptotic solution and a power series. Since the potential energy approaches infinity as xx and the coordinate QQ approach infinity, the wavefunction must approach zero. The function that has this property and satisfies the differential equation for large values of xx is the exponential function

exp(−x22)(5.6.11)(5.6.11)exp⁡(−x22)

The general expression for a power series is

∑n=0∞cnxn(5.6.12)(5.6.12)∑n=0∞cnxn

This power series can be truncated after the first term, after the second term, after the third term, etc. to produce a set of polynomials. There is one polynomial for each value of vv where vv can be equal to any integer value including zero.

∑n=0vcnxn(5.6.13)(5.6.13)∑n=0vcnxn

Each of the truncations of the power series in Equation 5.6.135.6.13 can be multiplied by the exponential function in Equation 5.6.115.6.11 to create a family of valid solutions to the differential equation.

ψv(x)=∑n=0vcnxnexp(−x22)(5.6.14)(5.6.14)ψv(x)=∑n=0vcnxnexp(−x22)

While polynomials in general approach ∞ (or -∞) as x approaches ∞, the decreasing exponential term overpowers the polynomial term so that the overall wavefunction exhibits the desired approach to zero at large values of x or -x. The exact forms of polynomials that solve Equation 5.6.105.6.10 are the Hermite polynomials, which are standard mathematical functions known from the work of Charles Hermite. In completing Exercise 5.6.95.6.9, you should have noticed that as the quantum number increases and becomes very large, the probability distribution approaches that of a classical oscillator. This observation is very general. It was first noticed by Bohr, and is called the Bohr Correspondence Principle. This principle states that classical behavior is approached in the limit of large values for a quantum number. A classical oscillator is most likely to be found in the region of space where its velocity is the smallest. This situation is similar to walking through one room and running through another. In which room do you spend more time? Where is it more likely that you will be found?
